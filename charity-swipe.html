01 — Overview

The design tension nobody asked about

Dating apps figured out something powerful: the swipe is the lowest-friction decision a human can make on a phone. Right or left. Yes or no. It's binary, it's fast, and it's deeply satisfying.

So here's the uncomfortable question: can you take a mechanic engineered for romantic dopamine hits and repurpose it for charitable giving without it feeling gross? That was the entire project.

Challenge
Persuasive mechanics from dating apps can feel manipulative in a charitable context
The same patterns that keep people swiping at 2am on Hinge could trivialize the act of giving. We needed to borrow the usability without importing the dark patterns.
Goal
Make giving feel as natural as swiping — without exploiting goodwill
Low friction, high intention. The swipe should surface organizations efficiently, but every interaction should encourage the user to actually read, think, and choose deliberately.
02 — Research

Understanding how people actually give

Before touching Figma, we spent the first six weeks on research. Competitive analysis of GiveDirectly, Charity Navigator, GoFundMe. User interviews with 12 participants aged 22-35 — the demographic most likely to use swipe-based apps and least likely to have an established charitable giving habit.

The key finding was surprising: people don't avoid donating because they don't care. They avoid it because the discovery process feels overwhelming. Too many organizations, not enough trust signals, and no way to casually browse without committing to a 20-minute research session.

"I want to give money to good causes but every charity website looks like it was designed in 2009 and I don't know who to trust."
— Research participant, 26
Insight
Discovery is the bottleneck, not generosity
9 of 12 participants said they'd donate more if they had a trusted, low-effort way to find organizations aligned with their values.
Insight
Familiarity breeds comfort, not contempt
Participants immediately understood the swipe paradigm. The mental model was already there — we just needed to redirect it toward a different kind of connection.
03 — Design

Five core screens, one interaction model

Every screen was designed around a single question: does this reduce friction toward a meaningful connection between user and organization? If it just looked cool but didn't serve that goal, we cut it.

🔍
Explore Page
Discovery-first landing that surfaces organizations matching user interests. Algorithm-driven but transparent — users always know why something is being shown to them. No black-box "for you" feeds.
Discovery UX
🔎
Search + Filters
Cause-based filtering (environment, education, health, mutual aid), location radius, and organization size. Designed for users who already know what they care about and want to skip the browsing phase.
Information Architecture
🏛️
Organization Profile
Mission statement, impact metrics, financial transparency score, and volunteer/donate CTAs. We spent a lot of time on the trust signals here — star ratings felt reductive, so we went with verified impact data and third-party audit badges.
Content Design
👤
User Profile
Giving history, saved organizations, and preference tuning. No public leaderboards or social comparison — we deliberately avoided gamifying generosity into a competitive sport.
Personalization
👆
Swipe Interaction
The core mechanic. Swipe right to save, left to skip. But we added a deliberate slowdown: each card shows enough information that the swipe is an informed choice, not a reflex. Card height was intentionally tall to prevent rapid-fire swiping.

04 — The Swipe

Designing friction on purpose

The whole point of a swipe is speed. So why would we slow it down? Because speed without intention is just noise. In dating apps, a fast swipe means you're filtering on appearance. In a charity context, a fast swipe means you're not reading — and if you're not reading, you're not choosing.

01
Card content density
Each swipe card shows the org name, cause category, a one-line mission, and one key impact stat. Enough to make an informed decision. Not so much that it becomes a wall of text.
02
Swipe threshold tuning
We increased the minimum swipe distance by 40% compared to typical dating app patterns. This prevents accidental swipes and forces a slightly more deliberate gesture — small enough that it doesn't feel clunky, big enough that it's never an accident.
03
No infinite scroll
Batches of 10 organizations. After each batch, a brief summary of what you saved and a prompt to explore those further. The deck runs out on purpose — giving shouldn't feel like an endless feed.
04
Undo without shame
Single-tap undo on accidental skips. No "Super Swipe" upsells, no premium features gating basic interactions. The monetization model was never about selling swipes.
05 — The Ethics Question

Where design meets responsibility

Every design review came back to the same question: are we helping people give, or are we making them feel like they're giving? The distinction matters more than you'd think.

Tension
Persuasion vs manipulation
Dating apps optimize for addiction. Charity apps should optimize for intention. We drew the line at anything that made the user feel guilty for not engaging — no push notifications guilt-tripping about "organizations waiting for you."
Tension
Swipe fatigue
If users swipe through 50 orgs in a minute, are they actually choosing? The batch system and deliberate card density were our answer. Slower swiping, better matches.
Tension
Gamification boundaries
Streaks, badges, and points can cheapen the act of giving. We chose meaning over metrics. Your profile shows impact — "you helped fund 3 clean water projects" — not "you're on a 14-day streak."
Tension
Trust signals
Users need to trust that their engagement leads to real impact. Every org profile links to third-party audits, shows financial breakdowns, and displays verified impact data. Transparency was non-negotiable.
06 — Reflection

What five months of ethical design taught me

CharitySwipe was a 5-month collaborative project that forced the team to think deeply about what we owe the people using the things we design. It's easy to say "don't use dark patterns." It's harder to define where a helpful nudge ends and a manipulative push begins.

"The hardest part wasn't making it feel like a dating app. It was making sure it didn't feel like one in the wrong ways."
— Andre Espinoza
⚖️
Ethics as design constraint
When persuasive mechanics meet charitable giving, ethics isn't an afterthought — it's the primary constraint that shapes every interaction, every copy decision, every animation timing.
🤝
Remote collaboration at scale
Five months of remote collaboration with a multi-person team. Explicit design decisions, shared Miro boards, weekly critique sessions. The process was as designed as the product.
🔄
Familiar does not mean appropriate
Just because a pattern works in one context doesn't mean it translates. Every interaction we borrowed from dating apps needed re-evaluation through a completely different ethical lens.
